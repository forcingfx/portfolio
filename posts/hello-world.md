I receive between 20 and 100 emails weekly; in 2024, I sent 76 emails weekly. Very few people would admit to receiving and sending so few emails—folks almost proudly wear red unread badges like medals on their phone home screens.

Yet, I manage teams spanning two locations and fifty-plus people, serving millions of customers annually, and don’t skip a beat. The problem has never been the volume of email or email itself, but rather the systems and protocols we use to communicate. Today, I'll share some of my favorite approaches to tuning your protocols to go beyond "inbox zero" and become a more effective communicator.

In 2007, I came across David Allen's "Getting Things Done" and Merlin Mann's "Inbox Zero" talk for the first time. That book and talk fundamentally influenced how I work. At the time, sending work-related emails was the primary means of corporate communication. I remember setting up an Exchange Server at my first job to "professionalize" email in a small organization of 30 students and staff. Email has always fascinated me for a few reasons:

1. Despite the onslaught of companies and tools such as Notion, JIRA, or Slack over the past decade, it remains the backbone of corporate communications today.
2. It still commands its own "etiquette" and social norms regarding response times, writing styles, and the types of work that should or should not get done over email.
3. Most people claim they receive too much email, and many enjoy sharing with others how much email they receive as a sign of status, importance, and productivity ("I'm busy, I'm valuable, look at how much email I get").

Over the intervening 18 years, things have changed. Most people fall into two camps today regarding email:

- They grew up with email as a primary means of communication and have learned to adapt to it using their own methods, accepting the medium's inherent nature.
- When they are first introduced to email in the context of social media, video, and messaging apps, they struggle to understand it. They don't accept the medium's nature and avoid it.

For the former, it's valuable to review the systems you have in place today and regain focus. For the latter, it can be helpful to understand how people who successfully use email as a medium do it and what systems they use to master it.

Mastering email = mastering communication

I will get to simple fixes, such as using filters and shortcuts effectively. Still, the overarching principle of mastering email is that email merely reflects the quality of your communication protocols.

If your company's culture is centered on resolving issues and making decisions through lengthy email threads, then you can change how decisions are debated and made.

Suppose you find yourself getting dozens of internal and external requests for your time and attention on new topics. In that case, you have an opportunity to review how you make yourself available to others.

If your team creates content (e.g., defining requirements, discussing designs, analyzing data) over email, you can streamline how those activities happen and how outputs are organized.

In all three cases (and many more), using email as a medium reflects some inefficiency in your company's communication protocols.

Three levels of communication

Here's an exercise for you to try this week: For every email that you read and send, reflect briefly on whether it falls into one of the following buckets:

Broadcasts: Announcements, status updates, newsletters, reports, and external solicitations such as vendor pitches or partnership inquiries.

Collaboration: Brainstorming sessions, design reviews, project planning, requests for input and review of proposals, and requests for resources.

Decisions: Approvals, escalations, and clarifications of priorities.

Email is great for broadcasts, terrible for collaboration, and acceptable for making decisions (with some rules I'll address below). The very first set of steps you can take to use email more effectively is to move certain types of communication to more appropriate media:

Continue sending broadcasts over email, ensuring that most people will read them. They are also easier to write in narrative form, and tracking status in tools can be cumbersome (I have tested and failed with OKR tracking tools; they don't work).

Shift collaboration to other tools - virtual whiteboards, collaborative docs with comments, and live meetings for high-bandwidth conversations.

Make decisions using a clear decision-making template and solicit input by calling a decision meeting (for important decisions). I'll go into this in more detail in future editions.

The same categorization helps you corral and process incoming email more effectively: Archive broadcasts once read (and if they require a response or action, either do it immediately or place it in your to-do list), conduct collaboration outside of email (move conversations to a doc if they aren't there already), and demand folks follow your established process for making decisions if a decision needs to be made.

What remains are emails requesting a response or input - a question from the CEO, a request from another team, or a notification from a system (such as a collaboration tool). To address these issues, we must review the systems we use to process email.

Reviewing best practices for processing email

Most of the tactics below should be known to you. However, I'll wager that every reader will find at least one tactic that they aren't using or haven't used in a long time.

Use filters. Use your email application’s ability to filter messages and review messages that tend to be repeated broadcasts or broadcasts you cannot unsubscribe from and don't read. Apply a filter to either auto-archive those or move them to a folder for batch reviewing at a later time. Review your filters monthly by revisiting your archive, checking which emails were missed (or not) and could have been filtered out of view, and adjust the filters accordingly.

Unsubscribe aggressively. Returning to your Archive, search for emails containing the word "unsubscribe". Review them and unsubscribe from any services you no longer need. Generally, you'll have two reactions to this:

There's this one newsletter I really like, and should keep

There's this update I'm subscribed to, and never read, but want to keep

I never signed up for this, nor did I expect to.

For (c), the action is clear: Unsubscribe. For (a), challenge yourself: when in doubt, unsubscribe. You can always resubscribe later. If you are worried that you'll forget about a specific newsletter, then it either wasn't providing value in the first place, or you can opt to track it as a to-do to revisit later.

Learn a few, not all, keyboard shortcuts. The most common action you will have for an email is to archive it. In Gmail, the only three shortcuts you need to know to increase your processing time by 3x are:

"E" for archive

"J" and "K" for moving up and down your inbox.

"A" to reply to all

You can find more shortcuts here, but I highly advise learning the first three and spending a week using them.

Process email in batches, and always to zero. Check your email two or at most three times a day: Morning, noon (optional), and end of day. When processing email, never leave your inbox without having processed everything. This means that when viewing an email, you decide to:

Archive/delete it

Create an action in your to-do list, then archive/delete it

Snooze it ("defer it", in David Allen's parlance).

Make it hard to access email. To be effective at processing to zero, a common pitfall is constantly revisiting email. Remove the email app from your phone's home screen (even better: delete it, but this may not be feasible for everyone and can introduce more friction than it removes). Remove it from your Windows pinned taskbar or macOS dock, and hide the dock/taskbar when running so you're not compelled to switch to it constantly. Quit the app when not processing email. Turn off email notifications.

Set up VIP notifications. Your FOMO is already screaming at me. "What if my boss sends me an email at 2 am!". If that's important to you, set up notifications for specific VIPs. Gmail and Outlook both have the capability to define specific people as VIPs and set notifications just for those.

Use AI liberally. I find it challenging to recommend specific tools here, as each organization has its own unique constraints when using AI tools, and you should exercise caution when sharing confidential information with third parties. Utilize your company's sanctioned AI platforms to compose and refine emails. I have enjoyed using Superhuman as an email client for this purpose, but with numerous plugins and tools available, I’ll reserve a recommendation until the market matures further.

A strange side effect I noticed from turning off notifications was that I ended up checking email more frequently because I had anxiety about missing important messages. It required me to block my email app at certain times of the day on my phone to conquer this. Managing screen time, though,  is a topic worthy of a separate essay.
